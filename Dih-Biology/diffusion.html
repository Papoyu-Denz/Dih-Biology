All About Diffusion

What is Diffusion?

Diffusion

Although you may not know what diffusion is, you’ve likely experienced it. Imagine walking through your front door and smelling a pleasant aroma from the kitchen. That smell reached you due to the diffusion of molecules through the air.

Diffusion is defined as the net movement of molecules from an area of greater concentration to an area of lesser concentration.

Molecules in gases, liquids, and solids are always in motion due to their kinetic energy. These random movements and collisions cause molecules to gradually move from densely packed areas to areas with fewer molecules.

This process forms a concentration gradient, an uneven distribution of molecules. Over time, as molecules continue to spread out, they reach a dynamic equilibrium—a state where they are evenly distributed but still in motion.

Diffusion happens in both living and nonliving systems. In living organisms, it enables the movement of essential substances like oxygen, carbon dioxide, and nutrients across cell membranes.

The Science Behind Diffusion

The Science Behind Diffusion

Diffusion is the net movement of anything—atoms, ions, molecules, or even energy— from a region of higher concentration to one of lower concentration. It is driven by a gradient in Gibbs free energy or chemical potential.

While diffusion typically moves particles "downhill" along the concentration gradient, rare scenarios such as spinodal decomposition involve "uphill" diffusion, where movement occurs from a region of lower concentration to higher.

A Stochastic Process

Diffusion is inherently a stochastic process, meaning it involves randomness in the movement of particles. Because of this, diffusion models are widely used in fields such as statistics, probability theory, information theory, neural networks, finance, and even marketing.

Widespread Applications

Beyond physics and biology, the principle of diffusion applies to diverse areas including chemistry, sociology, economics, data science, and more. Whether it’s particles, ideas, or prices—everything tends to spread out from areas of high concentration.

Understanding Gradients

A gradient represents a change in value over distance—like concentration, pressure, or temperature. These are termed concentration gradients, pressure gradients, or temperature gradients respectively.

Fun fact: The word "diffusion" comes from the Latin word diffundere, which means "to spread out."

Osmosis and Diffusion

Diffusion and Osmosis

Diffusion and osmosis are both types of passive transport. Thus, both of them occur in a downhill manner and without energy expenditure. The difference is the diffusing molecules or particles. In diffusion, the diffusing particles are the solutes of a solution. In osmosis, the diffusing particles are the solvent of the solution, i.e. water molecules. In osmosis, water molecules diffuse from an area of high water concentration to an area of low water concentration across a biological membrane. Water that is drawn towards a concentrated solution but does not involve a biological membrane is not osmosis. Whenever the fingers are placed in water for a longer time, they become pruney because of the water flow inside the cells.
